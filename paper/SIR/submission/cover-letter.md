Dear Editor,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript, **"Quantifying Hidden Exploitation: Dual-Method Prevalence Estimates of Labour Exploitation among Domestic Workers in the United Kingdom"**, for consideration in *Social Indicators Research*.

The paper responds to a persistent gap in the SDG 8.7 monitoring architecture: the populations most affected by labour exploitation — undocumented migrants, live-in domestic workers, tied-visa workers, agricultural day labourers — are systematically absent from the household and labour-force surveys that produce official social indicators. Existing prevalence estimates for modern slavery therefore rely on multiple-systems estimation from administrative case data, or on qualitative and civil-society case documentation. Neither approach yields the population-representative estimates that the SDG 8.7 target requires. The paper develops and demonstrates a replicable measurement design that addresses this gap through matched ego/alter survey instrumentation for hidden populations reachable through civil-society networks.

Two contributions justify submission to *Social Indicators Research* specifically. First, we apply respondent-driven sampling (RDS) and the network scale-up method (NSUM) to the same survey instrument administered to the same 85 respondents, eliciting ego-based and alter-based estimates of an identical set of five ILO-aligned indicators. To our knowledge this is among the first applications of a dual RDS + NSUM design to modern-slavery prevalence in any developed-economy setting. The design produces two complementary estimands — own-experience prevalence from RDS, network-visible prevalence from NSUM — and where they diverge, the gap is itself diagnostic evidence about which exploitation behaviours are observable across workers' personal networks and which remain concealed. This "visibility gradient" is a substantive social-indicator finding in its own right and has direct policy implications for network-mediated outreach programmes.

Second, we operationalise modern slavery as a graded formative indicator framework rather than a binary condition, drawing on the ILO's own eleven-indicator framework and its associated survey-guidelines handbook. The design supports both indicator-level prevalence estimation for the five binary measures and a continuous formative-additive composite risk index across all eleven ILO indicators. The composite is anchored on the ILO severity ordering and is shown to be essentially weight-invariant under $\pm 50\%$ perturbation of the sub-indicator weights (Spearman $\rho = 0.992$). This gives *SIR* readers a measurement design that generalises to other hidden populations relevant to SDG 8.7 monitoring, with the substantive UK migrant-domestic-worker case as a hard, realistic testbed rather than as the endpoint of the paper.

We are careful throughout to characterise what the design does and does not license. The order-of-magnitude divergence between the RDS (35–84%) and MBSU NSUM Moderate (1–3%) estimates is interpreted as diagnostic rather than as competing estimates of a single quantity, and the paper explicitly does *not* claim a definitive national prevalence figure. Robustness of the substantive ordering of indicators is documented across four methodologically unrelated estimators (RDS-I, RDS-SS, MA-RDS, MBSU NSUM), across four assumed population sizes (50K, 100K, 980K, 1.74M), and against a leave-one-out exclusion of the single longest recruitment chain (seed #55, 12 respondents). All sensitivity analyses are reported in the Online Appendix.
