# Drafts: revised introduction — surgical edits

The current introduction (paragraphs 11-12) was written for an industrial-relations audience and opens with "As industrial relations scholars, we have been called to rethink our analytical practices..." For SIR — a quality-of-life and social-indicators journal — that opening signals the wrong disciplinary register and buries the measurement contribution.

Five surgical edits below, listed in order of severity. I'd recommend applying all five, but each is independent and can be accepted/rejected.

## Edit 1 (MUST) — Replace the opening sentence of paragraph 11

**Current:**
> As industrial relations scholars, we have been called to rethink our analytical practices and adjust our approach to better reflect transformations in the representation and self-organisation of migrant workers (Alberti and Però, 2018).

**Proposed:**
> Modern slavery — forced labour, debt bondage, document and identity-document withholding, and related coercive practices — is named under Sustainable Development Goal Target 8.7 as a priority for elimination by 2030. Reliable prevalence measurement is a precondition for monitoring that target, yet the populations most affected are largely invisible to the household and labour-force surveys on which official social indicators rely.

**Why:** Opens the paper on SIR's home turf (SDG monitoring, social-indicator measurement of a quality-of-life-relevant phenomenon) rather than on industrial-relations scholarly identity. Names the problem before the methodology.

## Edit 2 (MUST) — Cut the IR-frame middle of paragraph 11

**Current (sentences 2-4 of paragraph 11):**
> Member-led civil society and third sector organisations have been described as the 'new actors' in the industrial relations of these workers (Alberti and Cutter, 2022). Such organisations work alongside workers and employers to resist any downgrading of employment standards. Central to their endeavors is identification of the nature and scale of employment rights abuses, often discussed in terms of labour exploitation (see for example Kalayaan, 2008; Latin American Women's Rights Service, 2023).

**Proposed:**
> Conventional household surveys underestimate prevalence in this domain by an order of magnitude or more, both because the populations affected (undocumented migrants, live-in domestic workers, sex workers, agricultural day labourers) are excluded from sampling frames, and because exploitation behaviours — especially those at the more severe end of the spectrum — are typically not self-reported even when the respondent is contacted. The civil-society and grey-literature evidence base on UK domestic workers (Kalayaan 2008; Latin American Women's Rights Service 2023) is qualitatively rich but does not yield population-level prevalence estimates suitable for SDG monitoring or comparison across populations.

**Why:** Keeps the civil-society references (they're real and substantive) but reframes them as motivation for needing better measurement, not as the central object of the paper. Establishes the gap our paper fills.

## Edit 3 (MUST) — Replace paragraph 11's closing methodological summary

**Current (last 2 sentences of paragraph 11):**
> Recently, more serious labour rights abuse has come to the fore with studies in fields such as operations management examining exploitation under the umbrella term 'modern slavery' (Gold, Trautrims and Trodd, 2015; New, 2015; Benstead, Hendry and Stevenson, 2018; Stevenson and Cole, 2018). In this article, we use the case of migrant domestic workers in the UK to demonstrate how academics can work with civil society actors representing similarly hard-to-reach workers in this, and other national and sectoral contexts, to make use of prevalence estimators calculated using quantitative methodologies, namely respondent driven sampling and network scale-up methods, to estimate the prevalence and assess the degree of sub-standard employment practices to support their advocacy for immigration policy improvements.

**Proposed:**
> The International Labour Organization's eleven indicators of forced labour (ILO 2012, 2017) provide an operational framework for measuring modern slavery, but the empirical question of *which* indicators are observable through *which* survey design remains open. Studies typically apply one design — household survey, respondent-driven sampling (RDS), or the network scale-up method (NSUM) — and report prevalence accordingly. None to our knowledge applies both RDS and NSUM to the same survey instrument for the same indicators, the design that would allow direct triangulation of ego-based against alter-based estimates and, where the two diverge, diagnostic inference about which exploitation behaviours are visible within personal networks and which are not.

**Why:** Drops the OSCM citations (Gold, New, Benstead, Stevenson) which belong to a different conversation than SIR. Telegraphs the dual-method contribution. Names the measurement gap concretely.

## Edit 4 (RECOMMEND) — Rewrite paragraph 12

**Current (entire paragraph 12, 119 words):**
> Specific concerns about high levels of exploitation among domestic workers persist in the business ethics and grey literature (Kalayaan, 2008; Mantouvalou, 2016; Varman et al., 2021; Latin American Women's Rights Service, 2023), yet to our knowledge no-one has yet worked with these organisations to estimate the nature and extent of the risk of labour exploitation and abuse that may exist among domestic workers in the UK. Specifically, this article aims to contribute to a more nuanced understanding of how civil society actors in the UK may engage with quantitative methodologies to improve understanding of the realities of domestic workers' conditions by demonstrating the use of a statistically robust estimation of the nature and proportion of labour exploitation and abuse.

**Proposed:**
> This paper makes two contributions. First, we apply RDS and NSUM to the same survey instrument with the same respondents, eliciting ego-based and alter-based estimates of an identical set of five ILO-aligned indicators. This dual-method design lets the two estimators triangulate prevalence, and where they diverge, exposes the visibility gradient of different exploitation behaviours within workers' personal networks. Second, we operationalise modern slavery as a graded hierarchy of indicators rather than a binary condition, arranged from a low bar (excessive working hours, common and harder to interpret as coercion) through to a high bar (document and passport withholding, unambiguously coercive). This hierarchy lets a single survey instrument capture exploitation across the full spectrum of severity and supports a continuous risk index for analyses that need one. We demonstrate the approach with a respondent-driven sample of 85 UK migrant domestic workers, the hardest realistic case for measurement in a developed-economy setting; the methodology generalises to other hidden populations relevant to SDG 8.7 monitoring.

**Why:** This is the central pitch paragraph. Names the two contributions explicitly, the n=85 sample size up front (rather than burying it), and the SDG 8.7 generalisation framing. Mirrors the new abstract and the contribution paragraph downstream so the reader sees the same two contributions repeated and reinforced through the paper.

## Edit 5 (NICE-TO-HAVE) — Add a one-paragraph signposting block

**Proposed new paragraph (insert as new paragraph 13, after the rewritten 12):**
> The remainder of the paper is organised as follows. Section 2 conceptualises labour exploitation as a graded latent construct measured by five ILO-aligned indicators arranged hierarchically by severity. Section 3 describes the matched-instrument survey design (Section 3.1) and the dual sampling approach combining RDS and NSUM (Section 3.2). Section 4 presents prevalence estimates by indicator and method, including frequentist RDS-I/RDS-II/RDS-SS estimators (Section 4.1), Bayesian Model-Assisted inference (Section 4.2), and NSUM with continuous-τ sensitivity analysis (Section 4.3). Section 5 reads the RDS-NSUM divergence as diagnostic evidence about visibility within workers' personal networks and discusses implications for SDG 8.7 monitoring.

**Why:** SIR papers commonly have a signposting paragraph at the end of the intro; it makes the paper easy to navigate for a reviewer who reads abstract → intro → conclusion first. Section numbers will need verification against the actual structure once intro edits land.
